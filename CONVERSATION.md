### Polyphonic Encoding in the GMRTI
Polyphonic Encoding is defined as the deliberate provision of multiple, structurally independent edge-paths to the same semantic destination within a single transmission act. In everyday terms, it is **redundancy**.

Within the GMRTI framework, redundancy is not viewed as a stylistic failure or imprecision. Instead, it is an epistemically necessary response to the metaagnostic condition. Because a transmitter cannot guarantee that any single path will survive being reconstructed in the receiver's mind, sending the same concept through multiple independent routes increases the probability that at least one path will arrive completely intact.

### Key Applications and Requirements
Polyphonic Encoding is the GMRTI's primary proactive strategy for ensuring transmission fidelity. Its necessity scales depending on the topological depth of the transmission:
- **Surface Subgraphs:** Polyphonic Encoding is useful but not mandatory, as failures here are usually easy to repair with simple clarification.
- **Structural Subgraphs:** Polyphonic Encoding is highly beneficial here to reduce the probability of a total reconstruction failure.
- **Axiomatic Edge-Clusters:** Polyphonic Encoding is **mandatory** at this foundational layer. Transmitters often fail to encode axiomatic claims redundantly because they treat them as obvious background knowledge, making this the most dangerous gap in spontaneous communication.

The framework notes that Polyphonic Encoding is already structurally present in many effective communication domains, such as internet packet protocols, pedagogical methods (e.g., providing a definition, an example, and an analogy), and legal drafting.

### The Failure Mode: Redundancy-Masking
The critical vulnerability of Polyphonic Encoding is a phenomenon called **Redundancy-Masking**.

This occurs when a successful, multi-path transmission successfully routes around a "Severe Edge Deficit" (a complete topological void in the receiver's understanding) without actually filling it. This produces a "false comosí"—an illusion of perfect understanding sustained only by the backup routes. If conditions change and those redundant paths are no longer available, the communication will catastrophically fail.

To counter Redundancy-Masking, the GMRTI prescribes performing at least one targeted probe using a *stripped*, single-route version of the transmission to ensure the reception was genuinely robust and not merely sustained by redundancy.

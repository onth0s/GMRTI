**R5 — The Edge-Taxonomy Economy Problem** `[OPEN]`

The Edge Taxonomy introduced in Section 2a.5 may itself become too elaborate for routine use. A taxonomy that is too coarse hides divergence by collapsing meaningfully distinct failure modes; a taxonomy that is too fine prevents adoption by making the classification task itself a source of overhead and divergence.

The current eight-type partition was introduced as a working hypothesis, not as the result of systematic investigation. Its boundaries are contested — inferential versus causal is a live dispute in philosophy of science; affective versus normative is contested in moral psychology; structural is a residual category that may conceal important internal distinctions. At the same time, eight types may already be too many for the Covenant Declaration step to use practically: requiring a transmitter to classify every edge among eight options before beginning communication imposes exactly the kind of upfront overhead that JIT-OM was designed to avoid.

**Open questions:** What is the minimum useful partition of edge-types — the coarsest taxonomy that still catches the most consequential misattribution failures? Is the inferential/causal distinction the single most important cut, given its prevalence across disciplines? Should the taxonomy be tiered — a coarse primary partition used in Covenant Declaration, with finer distinctions available via JIT-OM at detected misattribution sites — mirroring the JIT-OM approach to ontological marking? How does the edge-type taxonomy interact with the concept-type taxonomy: are certain edge-types more prevalent in certain concept-types, and if so, can the concept-type declaration implicitly constrain the expected edge-type, reducing the classification burden?
